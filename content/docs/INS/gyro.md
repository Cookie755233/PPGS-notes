title: 2. Gyro
---

{{< details title="Rigidity in space? To which instrument does it apply?" closed="true" >}}

A gyroscope remains in a fixed position in the plane in which it is spinning.
$\rightarrow$ **Attitude indicator** and **heading indicator**.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Precession? To which instrument does it apply?" closed="true" >}}

Precession: A force acting vertically upon an axis causes a turning moment 90 degrees ahead in the direction of rotation.
$\rightarrow$ **Turn coordinator**

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Gyro wanders" closed="true" >}}

* **Real wander**: An undesired precession caused by an imbalance of the gyro mass or friction in the bearing.
* **Apparent wander**: The earth rotates about its axis at 15°/hr, causing the gyro to appear to move to an observer on the earth. Aircraft transportation rate around the earth depends on **ground speed** and **latitude**.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Slipping turn? correction methods?" closed="true" >}}

A turn with **excessive** bank. The inclinometer ball moves **in** the direction of the bank (inside of the turn).

**Correction:**
1. Step on the rudder in the direction of the ball ("step on the ball").
2. **Increase** speed.
3. **Decrease** bank angle.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Skidding turn? correction methods?" closed="true" >}}

A turn with **insufficient** bank. The inclinometer ball moves to the **outside** of the bank (outside of the turn).

**Correction:**
1. Step on the rudder in the direction of the ball ("step on the ball").
2. **Decrease** speed.
3. **Increase** bank angle.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="How to '(re)activate' attitude gyro if it's not spinning properly?" closed="true" >}}

"Pull to cage" allows for a fast erection of the artificial horizon after engine start-up or in flight.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Higher the Latitude, ___ the mag var." closed="true" >}}

**Higher**

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="If all points on the Earth's surface that have the same variation are connected, a line results called an ___. The isogonal line connecting all points with a variation of 0 is ___ line." closed="true" >}}

If all points on the Earth's surface that have the same variation are connected, the line is called an **isogonic line**.
The isogonal line connecting all points with a variation of 0 is the **agonic line**.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Inclination angle? ___ to Latitude." closed="true" >}}

The angle between the magnetic field line and the horizontal component is the inclination angle (also known as **magnetic dip**).
The inclination angle is **proportional** to latitude.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Compass deviation?" closed="true" >}}

Compass deviation is produced by the local magnetic fields of the airplane's fuselage components and its electrical/electronic equipment.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="How does a compass overcome inclination?" closed="true" >}}

A compass adds a compensational dip-correcting counterweight, which depends on the latitude zone of operation.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Acceleration error?" closed="true" >}}

**Cause:**
The inclination (dip) error is adjusted using a compensation weight, creating an asymmetrical balance point.

**Errors (Northern Hemisphere):**
* When facing **East or West**, acceleration causes the compass heading to turn toward the **North**, and deceleration causes it to turn toward the **South** (ANDS).
* There is **no acceleration error** when facing directly North or South.
* *Note: The error acts opposite in the Southern Hemisphere.*

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Turning error?" closed="true" >}}

**Cause:**
The inclination error is adjusted using a compensation weight, which gets pulled by centrifugal force during a bank.

**Errors (Northern Hemisphere):**
* When facing **North**, turning left/right gives a momentary indication of a turn in the **opposite direction**.
* When facing **South**, turning left/right shows a turn in the correct direction but at a **faster rate** than actually being turned.
* When facing **East/West and turning North**, the compass will **lag** behind the actual heading.
* When facing **East/West and turning South**, the compass will **lead** ahead of the actual heading.
* *Note: The scenarios are opposite in the Southern Hemisphere (UNOS).*

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="What would happen when you're on HDG 210, and perform a right turn to 240? (N.H)" closed="true" >}}

Facing a southerly heading (210) and turning right to 240:
The compass will **lead** the turn by about 10 to 20 degrees once the turn is established. Therefore, the pilot should roll out of the turn when the compass indicates around **260** (240 + 20) and wait for the overshooting compass to rotate back to 240 after leveling the wings.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Flux valve" closed="true" >}}

A flux valve (flux gate) is used as an external directional reference to automatically adjust the Heading Indicator (HI) so that pilots do not have to manually reset the gyro to the compass every 15 minutes. It is often installed far from interference, such as in the wingtip or vertical stabilizer.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="AHRS" closed="true" >}}

**Attitude Heading Reference System** uses Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS) solid-state sensors. 

It measures acceleration, rate of turn, and magnetic components relative to the three axes of the aircraft, converting them into attitude and direction information relative to the Earth (acting as a solid-state gyro system).

{{< /details >}}
