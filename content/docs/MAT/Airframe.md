### Load and Stresses

{{< details title="@Tension" closed="true" >}}
A tension tends to stretch a structural member.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Compression" closed="true" >}}
Compressive loads are the opposite of tensile loads and tend to shorten structural members.\
Components designed to resist compressive loads are known as STRUTS.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Shear" closed="true" >}}
Shear is a force which tends to slide one of the material over an adjacent face.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Torsion" closed="true" >}}
Torsion is a condition that describes the twisting of a body.
Torsion is the twisting of an object due to an applied TORQUE.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Bending" closed="true" >}}
Bending of the structure involves the two basic loadings:
- Tension as the outer edge stretches.
- Compression as the inner edge squeezes together.
(The force is perpendicular to the axis).
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Buckling" closed="true" >}}
Buckling occurs to thin sheet materials when they are subjected compressive forces.\
Buckling can cause the deformation of the object. \
(The force is parallel to the axis).
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Fatigue" closed="true" >}}
Fatigue is the concept of when a structure fails at a lesser load under continual reversals of loading than it would under a steadily applied load.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Differences between SHEAR and TENSION?" closed="true" >}}
Shear applies onto different surfaces while tension works on the same one.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Wing spars?" closed="true" >}}
Wing spars are built around one or more main load bearing members to absorb the stress of lift/drag
{{< /details >}}

### Type of Construction

{{< details title="@Fuselage construction" closed="true" >}}
Truss: a load of carrying frmae of meal tubes with a nonstressed covering.\
Monocoque: The complete stress is carried by outer skin. The bulkheads are only to give shape. \
Semi-monocoque: The load is carried by the outer skin and stringers on the inner side of the skin.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Disadvantages for monocoque." closed="true" >}}
Even slight damage to the skin can seriously weaken the structure.
{{< /details >}}

### Type of Material

{{< details title="@Pros and cons of composite materials" closed="true" >}}
PROS:\
- Lighter weight\
- Smooth and easily form complex curved or streamlined structure \(\rightarrow\) reduce drag\
- Corrosion resistant\
- Do not suffer from metal fatigue and crack growth (as metals do) 

CONS:\
- Lack of visual proof of damage - there is no obvious sign of damage after impact.\
- The potential for heat damage to the resin \(\rightarrow\) (white paint on composite is often used to minimize this Issue)\
- Chemical paint strippers are very harmful to composite \(\rightarrow\) (use gentle grid blasting or sanding)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@What's the benefits of the following materials and their alloys. Aluminium Steel Titanium" closed="true" >}}
Aluminum and its alloys are the most widely used metals for structural use due to a good strength to weight ratio.

Steel and its alloys are only used where strength is vital and weight penalty can be ignored.

Titanium is much lighter than steel and can be used where fire protection is required, i.e. firewalls. It has good strength and retains this and its corrosion resistance up to temperature of 400 degrees.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@GRP?" closed="true" >}}
Glass Reinforced Plastic. The plastics are epoxy or phenolic resin, but the strength comes from the embeded fibers. The epoxy resin is easy to handle and durable.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@CRP?" closed="true" >}}
Carbon-Reinforced Plastic. CRP have strength and stiffness with low specific weight. Very well designed carbon fiber structure can be lighter than equivalent aluminium structure by 30%.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@ARP?" closed="true" >}}
Aromatic Polyamides. This material is well known in aircraft construction
Kevlar is ideal for IMPACT RESISTANCE and flexibility, often used in cockpit, racing car, bullet-proof vest.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Fuel System components" closed="true" >}}
- Fuel tank/integral, container

- Fuel drain
- Filter/strainer
- Fuel boost pump
![Fuel system](/images/fuelsys.png)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="AVGAS 100 LL has a ___ coloring." closed="true" >}}
AVGAS 100 LL is dyed BLUE.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="AVGAS 100/130 is dyed in ___." closed="true" >}}
green.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="What does the numbers (e.g. AVGAS 100/130) mean?" closed="true" >}}
The two numbers (e.g., AVGAS 100/130) represent the fuel's octane ratings under two different engine conditions: Lean and Rich.\
The octane rating measures the fuel's ability to resist "knocking" or premature DETONATION in the engine.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Octane Rating?" closed="true" >}}
The octane number indicates the antiknock value of fuel against fuel knocking (detonation) .

The quality of a reference fuel is expressed in the percentage of: ISO-OCTANE and HEPTANE

This does not mean that the petro/ contains just iso-octane and heptane in these proportions, but that it has the same detonation resistance properties.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Primer pumps" closed="true" >}}
A manually operated pump (primer) which is used to pump fuel directly into the cylinders prior to engine start. \
The primer is useful in COLD weather when fuel in carburetor is difficult to vaporize.
![primer pump](/images/primerpump.png)

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="What is a spar valve" closed="true" >}}
What is a spar vale is an oil control valve allowing oil to be flow from the wing to engine near wing spars. It is also called a low pressure oil valve.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Fuel vent is pointed at ____ direction. Why?" closed="true" >}}
Fuel vent is pointed at which the aircraft is traveling. \
It is used to create a slight positive pressure in the fuel tank for better fuel flow, and prevent vapor lock happening.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="What does a fuel tank do?" closed="true" >}}
Each fuel tank is vented to allow air into the tank as volume of fuel decreases during operation and relieves excessive pressure due to fuel expansion.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Where does the check valve located? and where does it do?" closed="true" >}}
Check valve collects excess fuel from the fuel/air control unit back to the fuel reservoir tank. It is a one-way valve that prevents the fuel from flowing toward the opposite direction.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Why do we have 2 fuel pumps in fuel injection systems?" closed="true" >}}
Electrically driven pump is used to provides fuel under pressure to the fuel control unit for ENGINE STARTING and when ENGINE-DRIVEN FUEL PUMP is not available.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="What does a capacitor in fuel system do?" closed="true" >}}
It measures the amount of remaining fuel by comparing the electrical charge capacity of the fuel versus air. The amount of fuel is measured in VOLUME.

p.s. In bigger airplane, there will be a densitometer in the fuel tank to accurately calculate the amount of fuel into mass.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="A capacitor is directly proportional to ____ and inversely proportional ____." closed="true" >}}
A capacitor is directly proportional to SURFACE AREA and inversely proportional DISTANCE between the plates.

\(C \propto A/D\)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Why do we use full ___ mixture during take off?" closed="true" >}}
We use FULL RICH during take off because the fuel's octane ratings under full rich fuel-air mixture helps cool the engine and reduces the likelihood of detonation when the engine is operating at high power and high temperatures.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Why does vapor lock happen?" closed="true" >}}
Vapor lock happens when liquid fuel gets too hot and boils into a gas while still inside the fuel lines or pump. When happens, engine will stop and may be difficult to restart.
{{< /details >}}

### Exhaust Heat System

{{< details title="@How does cabin heating work?" closed="true" >}}
Air taken from the front of the cowling, flows THROUGH (does not directly mix with exhaust gas) a heat exchanger located on the exhaust stack and then to a mixer box, allows heated air to mix with cooler air. 

The control lever should be switched ON for the heated air to flow into the cabin area, and to set DEFROST to canopy or FLOOR to cabin floor.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Fuel tank venting" closed="true" >}}
Each fuel tank is vented to allow air into the tank as volume of fuel decreases during operation and relieves excessive pressure due to fuel expansion.
{{< /details >}}

### Landing Gear System

{{< details title="@Safety switch" closed="true" >}}
A safety switch (squat switch) on the main gear (OPENS the up circuit, which breaks the power supply to the gear motor when the airplane's weight compresses the Strut.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Shimmy Damper" closed="true" >}}
A shimmy damper is installed to suppress wobbling of the wheels during ground operation.\
The piston inside the cylinder is free to back and forth if it is moved slowly enough to allow fluid to transfer from one side of the piston to the other through a orifice.\
Since the orifice is very small, rapid movements of the piston occur during landing and takeoff are restricted and nose wheel Shimmy is eliminated.
![](/image/shimmydamper.png)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Shock struts/ Oleo struts" closed="true" >}}
The function of the shock strut is to absorb the impact of landing.\
The oleo struct consists of an enclosed cylinder including piston, oil, and air(N2).\
The shock of the landing impact is taken up by air spring and as oil is forced through a metering orifice (a restrict hole).\
The smaller shocks from taxiing are taken up by a cushion of compressed air.
{{< /details >}}

### Flight Control System

{{< details title="@Trim tab" closed="true" >}}
Trim tab is moved in the OPPOSITE direction to the control surface to help minimize your workload by aerodynamically maintain the surface in a desired position.
(It is essentially adjusting the length of the cable connecting to the trim tab)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Stabilator" closed="true" >}}
A one-piece of horizontal stabilizer that pivots from a central hinge point. \
They are extremely sensitive to control inputs. \
Anti-servo tabs are incorporated on the trailing edge to decrease sensitivity. They deflect in the SAME direction as the stabilator.
![](/images/stabilator.png)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Servo tab" closed="true" >}}
Only the servo tab moves in response to movement of the cockpit control.\
With the use of a servo tab, less force is needed to move the main control surface.
![](/images/servotab.png)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Balanced tab" closed="true" >}}
The linkage is designed in such a way that when the main control surface is moved, the tab moves in the OPPOSITE direction.\
Thus, the aerodynamic forces, acting on the tab, ASSIST in moving the main control surface.
![](/images/balancetab.png)
{{< /details >}}


{{< details title="@Anti-servo tabs" closed="true" >}}
Anti-servo tabs are incorporated on the trailing edge to DECREASE sensitivity. They move in the SAME direction as the stabilator.
![](/images/antiservo.png)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="If your plane has an right turning tendency, what should you do to the aileron trim tab?" closed="true" >}}
Right turning tendency\
\(\rightarrow\) Left wing has greater lift\
\(\rightarrow\) reduce lift by moving UP the AILERON\
\(\rightarrow\) The aileron trim tab moves in the opposite direction to the control surface\
\(\rightarrow\) hence adjust trim tab DOWN
{{< /details >}}
