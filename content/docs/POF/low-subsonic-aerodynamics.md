### Basic Airflow & Definitions

{{< details closed="true" title="The Relative Wind" >}}
The relative wind appears when relative motion exists between an object and the air.

A relative wind blows in an opposite direction to the flight path of a moving airplane.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Angle of Attack (AoA)" >}}
Angle of attack is an angle measured between chord-line and relative wind.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Angle of Incidence" >}}
Angle of incidence is an FIXED angle measured between chord line and longitudinal axis of the airplane.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Pitch Attitude Angle" >}}
It is an angle measured between aircraft longitudinal axis and the HORIZON, indicating an inclination of aircraft relative to the earth.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Lift and Drag Orientation" >}}
Lift is **perpendicular** to the relative wind; drag is **parallel** to flight path.
{{< /details >}}

### Airfoil Anatomy & Geometry

{{< details closed="true" title="6 Elements of an Airfoil" >}}
1. **The chordline**: It is a straight line connecting the leading and trailing edge of the airfoil, indicating an inclination of the airfoil.
2. **The chord**: It is the characteristic length of the airfoil.
3. **The mean-camber line**: It is a line halfway between the upper and lower surfaces. A camber indicates the displacement of mean camber line away from the chordline.
4. **The Maximum camber**: They are the characteristics of mean-camber line, and expressed in "percent of the chord".
5. **The maximum thickness**: Thickness determines the upper and lower surfaces of airfoils. Maximum thickness are expressed in 'percent of the chord'.
6. **Leading edge radius**: The leading edge radius describes the shape (how round or sharp) the front tip is expressed in 'percent of the chord'.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Aspect Ratio" >}}
It is the ratio of the wing span (length) to the chord (width) for a rectangular wing.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Taper Ratio" >}}
It is the tip chord to root chord (Ct/Cr), for plan-form with straight leading and trailing edges.

* **Cr (Root chord)** = Chord at the wing centerline
* **Ct (Tip chord)** = Chord at the tip
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Sweep Angle" >}}
It is the angle between the line of 25% chords and a perpendicular to the root chord.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Mean Aerodynamic Chord (MAC)" >}}
MAC is drawn on the airplane and its a parameter to indicate the location of C.G. (% MAC) for weight and balance, and longitudinal stability.
{{< /details >}}

### Wing Planforms

{{< details closed="true" title="Rectangular Wings" >}}
* (O) Easy to made
* (O) Good performance at low speed.
* (X) Stronger induced drag
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Tapered Wings" >}}
* (O) reduce drag by narrowing toward the tip
* (O) lighter structure
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Elliptical Wings" >}}
* (O) reduced more drag → faster maximum speed.
* (X) harder to manufacture
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Sweepback Wing Benefits & Trade-offs" >}}
* (O) Great performance at HIGH SPEED
* (O) Wind component passing longitudinal axis along the wing is smaller (W x cosθ), allows for greater airspeed before the wind reaches Mach 1, which may lead to exterior error.
* (x) Creates a thicker boundary layer on the wing due to the wind components perpendicular to the wing, causing it to stall from the wingtip. → would require slats on bigger airplanes.
* (x) Not enough lift on slow speed since the wind is splited into two components.
{{< /details >}}

### Lift & Stall Dynamics

{{< details title="Lift Equation" closed="true" >}}
\(L = q \times s \times C_{lift}\) which expands to \(L = \frac{1}{2} \times \rho \times V^2 \times C_{lift}\)

* \(q\) = dynamic pressure
* \(s\) = planform area
* \(C_{lift}\) = coefficient of lift related to AoA
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Center of Pressure (CP) & Lift Coefficient Movement" >}}
Lift is the component perpendicular to the relative wind; drag is parallel to the relative wind. The resultant force acts at the location is called "center of pressure (CP)"

C.L. moves **forward nose** when AoA is greater.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="How Pilot Controls Lift" >}}
1. Adjust airspeed → double the airspeed, fourfold the lift.
2. Adjust AoA
3. a combination of the above. (acceleration/deceleration)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Pilot Actions When Accelerating (At a Higher Speed)" >}}
\(L = q \times s \times C_{lift} \text{ (AoA)}\)

1. increase power (throttle forward)
2. decrease AoA (control wheel forward)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Stall Speed & Stall Angle" >}}
The stall speed happens when C_lift is **at max (C_lift_max)**.

Stall angle is **fixed** regardless of flight speed, attitude or weight.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Initial Buffet" >}}
Initial buffet occurs when the airflow starts to seperate at the TRAILING edge.
{{< /details >}}

### Drag Profiles

{{< details closed="true" title="Total Drag Equation" >}}
$$\text{Total Drag} = \text{Parasite Drag (Form + Interference + Skin Friction)} + \text{Induced Drag}$$
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Form Drag (Pressure Drag)" >}}
Form drag, also called "PRESSURE DRAG", is caused by the FLOW SEPARATION which lead to a pressure difference at the fron and rear part of object. 

The streamlines are displaced because the energy is consumed passing through corners and STATIC pressure is lowered on the rear side.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Skin Friction Drag" >}}
Skin friction drag is created in boundary layer, which has an airflow velocity of zero on the surface, of an aerodynamic body. 

The counteracting force in which slowing down the air in vicinity in the direction of the airflow is called skin friction drag.

Skin friction force results in warming of the outer skin of the body, and it would heat up as the speed increases.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Parasite Drag Pronouncement (Blunt vs. Streamline)" >}}
1. **Blunt body objects** → form (pressure) drag is most pronounced.
2. **Streamline body objects** → skin friction drag is most pronounced.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details closed="true" title="Interference Drag" >}}
Interference drag is any ADDITIONAL parasite drag resulting from the aerodynamic interaction of various components as the air flows around the body.
{{< /details >}}
