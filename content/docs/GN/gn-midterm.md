## Earth Structure & Coordinate Systems

{{< details title="@Great Circle" closed="true" >}}
The intersection of the Earth's surface and the plane which pass through the Earth's center.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Small Circle" closed="true" >}}
A small circle of a sphere is the circle constructed by a plane crossing the sphere not in its center. Small circles always have smaller diameter than the sphere itself.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Rhumb Line" closed="true" >}}
Rhumb line is a line on the earth surface which has a constant angle to the meridian.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Geographic North/South Pole" closed="true" >}}
Geographic North/South pole is a point where the Earth's axis of rotation intersects the Earth's surface.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Geocentric Latitude" closed="true" >}}
The angle at the center of earth between equator and parallel of Latitude.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Geodetic Latitude" closed="true" >}}
The Geodetic (or Geographic) Latitude is an angle between the normal line of the Earth's surface and the plane of the equator.
{{< /details >}}

## Direction & Magnetism

{{< details title="@Magnetic North Pole" closed="true" >}}
Magnetic North pole is the point on the Earth's surface at which the Earth's magnetic field points vertically downwards.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@True North" closed="true" >}}
Truth north is the direction of the geographic north pole measured from the earth's surface.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Magnetic North" closed="true" >}}
Magnetic north is a direction of local Earth's magnetic field.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Compass North" closed="true" >}}
Compass North is the direction in which a compass north end points.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Magnetic Variation" closed="true" >}}
The angle between True North and Magnetic North. If magnetic north is on the right side of true north, it is East variation.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Isogonic Line" closed="true" >}}
Is a line on earth that all points of this line have same magnetic variation.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Magnetic Deviation" closed="true" >}}
A compass error caused by magnetic disturbances from electrical and metal components in the airplane. The correction for this error is displayed on a compass correction card placed near the magnetic compass in the airplane.
{{< /details >}}

The speed of the aircraft as shown on the airspeed indicator.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Calibrated Airspeed" closed="true" >}}
Calibrated airspeed is equal to the airspeed indicator reading (IAS) corrected for pitot system position error. Usually, this error is small.

EAS is equal to calibrated airspeed corrected for compressibility of air.

True Air Speed is the speed of the aircraft relative to the air.

## Altimetry & Pressures

{{< details title="@Indicated Altitude" closed="true" >}}
Altitude measured by altimeter with proper altimeter setting.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Pressure Altitude" closed="true" >}}
Pressure altitude is the indicated altitude when altimeter setting is \(29.92 \text{ in}\) (\(1013.2 \text{ mb}\)).
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Density Altitude" closed="true" >}}
Density altitude is pressure altitude corrected for nonstandard temperature variation. It reflect an altitude in the standard atmosphere having the same air density.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@True Altitude" closed="true" >}}
Actual vertical distance above mean sea level.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Absolute Altitude" closed="true" >}}
Actual vertical distance above earth surface (height).
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@QNH" closed="true" >}}
Barometric pressure at sea level at your location. If QNH is set correctly, altimeter will indicate airport elevation when aircraft touch down.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@QNE" closed="true" >}}
Denotes the standard setting (\(29.92 \text{ in}\), \(1013 \text{ mb}\)). In high altitude, all the aircraft use this standard altimeter setting.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@QFE" closed="true" >}}
Airfield barometric pressure. If QFE is set correctly, altimeter will indicated zero altitude when aircraft is on the ground of airport.
{{< /details >}}

## 5 Special Use Areas

{{< details title="@1. Alert Areas" closed="true" >}}
Alert areas are shown on aeronautical charts to inform you of unusual types of aerial activities, such as parachute jumping and glider towing, or high concentrations of student pilot training.
{{< /details >}}


{{< details title="@2.Warning Areas" closed="true" >}}
Warning areas extend from three nautical miles outward from the coast of the United States and contain activity that may be hazardous to nonparticipating aircraft.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@3.Restricted Areas" closed="true" >}}
Restricted areas often have invisible hazards to aircraft, such as artillery firing, aerial gunnery, or guided missiles. Permission to fly through restricted areas must be granted by the controlling agency.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@4. Prohibited Areas" closed="true" >}}
Prohibited areas are established for security or other reasons associated with the national welfare and contain airspace within which the flight of aircraft is prohibited.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@5. Military Operations Area (MOA)" closed="true" >}}
A military operations area (MOA) is a block of airspace in which military operations are conducted. VFR aircraft are not prevented from flying through active MOAs, but it is wise to avoid them when possible.
{{< /details >}}

## Other Airspaces
{{< details title="@Temporary Flight Restrictions (TFR)" closed="true" >}}
Temporary flight restrictions (TFRs) are regulatory actions that temporarily restrict certain aircraft from operating within a defined area in order to protect persons or property in the air or on the ground.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Six Types of TFRs (ADVESE)" closed="true" >}}
Types of TFRs include those that address disaster and hazards, space flight operations, VIP movement, emergency air traffic rules, air shows and sporting events on a case-by-case basis, and major sporting events.

* **A**irshow events
* **D**isaster and hazards
* **V**IP movement
* **E**mergency air traffic rules
* **S**pace operation
* **E**vents - major sport events
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Air Defense Identification Zones (ADIZ)" closed="true" >}}
Air defense identification zones (ADIZs) are established to facilitate identification of aircraft in the vicinity of U.S. international airspace boundaries.
{{< /details >}}

## Engine & Mixture Operations

{{< details title="@Best Economy Mixture" closed="true" >}}
The best economy mixture setting may only be used up to a power setting of \(75\%\). In order to obtain the lowest specific fuel consumption at a particular power setting proceed as follows: Slowly pull the mixture control lever back toward LEAN until the engine starts to run roughly. Then push the mixture control lever forward just far enough to restore smooth running. At the same time the exhaust gas temperature (EGT) should reach a maximum.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Best Power Mixture" closed="true" >}}
The mixture can be set for maximum performance at all power settings. The mixture should first be set as for 'best economy'. The mixture should then be enriched until the exhaust gas temperature is approximately \(100^\circ\text{F}\) (\(55^\circ\text{C}\)) lower. This mixture setting produces the maximum performance for a given manifold pressure and is mainly used for high power settings (approximately \(75\%\)).
{{< /details >}}

## Aerodrome & Runway Markings

{{< details title="@Displaced Threshold" closed="true" >}}
When the landing area begins at the point on the runway other than the designated beginning of the runway. It can be used for taxi, takeoff, landing rollout, but landing is prohibited because there may be some obstructions at the end of the runway such as trees or buildings.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Blast Pad / Stopway Area" closed="true" >}}
The blast pad is an area where propeller or jet blast can dissipate without creating a hazard to others. The stopway area is paved so that in the event of a rejected takeoff, it cannot be used for landing, takeoff, or taxiing.
{{< /details >}}

## Radio Navigation Systems

{{< details title="@VOR Test Facilities (VOT)" closed="true" >}}
VOR test facilities (VOTs) enable you to make precise VOR accuracy checks regardless of your airplane's position in relation to the facility because VOTs broadcast a signal for only one radial — \(360^\circ\). 

First, obtain the frequency from the chart supplement, tune your VOR receiver, and identify the VOT signal; you should hear a series of dots or a continuous tone. Next, set a course of either \(0^\circ\) or \(180^\circ\) on the VOR indicator. 

* If you set \(0^\circ\), the CDI should center with a **FROM** indication. 
* If you set \(180^\circ\), the CDI should center with a **TO** indication. 

If the CDI does not center, you can determine the magnitude of the error by rotating the OBS until the needle moves to the center position. The new course should be within \(\pm 4^\circ\) of your test course (\(0^\circ\) or \(180^\circ\)).
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@NOTAM(D)" closed="true" >}}
NOTAM(D) information is disseminated for all navigational facilities that are part of the U.S. airspace system and all public use airports, seaplane bases and heliports listed in the Chart Supplements.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@FDC NOTAM" closed="true" >}}
FDC NOTAMs, issued by the National Flight Data Center, contain regulatory information, such as TFRs or amendments to aeronautical charts.(TFR: Temporary Flight Restriction)
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Distance Measuring Equipment (DME)" closed="true" >}}
An equipment (airborne and ground) to measure, in nautical miles, the slant range distance of an aircraft from the navigation aid.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring (RAIM)" closed="true" >}}
Stands for receiver autonomous integrity monitoring. It is a software function in the GPS receiver to detect, deselect a failed satellite and give pilot warning.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@DME Arc Execution Steps" closed="true" >}}
1. Lead your turn onto the arc about \(1/2\) nautical mile prior to reaching the arc. Continue turning to the heading shown at the wingtip reference. 
2. Turn to a heading that puts the bearing pointer \(5^\circ\) to \(10^\circ\) ahead of the wingtip, and hold that heading until the bearing pointer moves \(5^\circ\) to \(10^\circ\) behind the wingtip. 
3. Visualize the wind direction on the compass card or RMI, and correct accordingly. If correcting away from the VOR, the bearing pointer moves behind the wingtip. If correcting toward the VOR, the pointer moves ahead of the wingtip.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="@ADS-B" closed="true" >}}
ADS-B equipped aircraft transmit their GPS positions to ATC, which results in much better accuracy and coverage than traditional radar.
{{< /details >}}


## Preflight Planning & Regulations

{{< details title="Preflight Action (14 CFR 91.103)" closed="true" >}}

For a flight under IFR or a flight not in the vicinity of an airport, preflight action as required by regulation shall include a review of what specific information (NW KRAFT):

- **N**OTAMs
- **W**eather reports and forecasts
- **K**now ATC traffic delays
- **R**unway lengths at the airports of intended use
- **A**lternatives available if the planned flight cannot be completed
- **F**uel requirements
- **T**akeoff and landing distance data
{{< /details >}}

## Navigation Methods

{{< details title="Three Common Ways to Navigate" closed="true" >}}

To navigate successfully, pilots must know their approximate position at all times or be able to determine it whenever they wish. Position may be determined by:

- Pilotage (by reference to visible landmarks);
- Dead reckoning (by computing direction and distance from a known position);
- Radio navigation (by use of radio aids).
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Pilotage Navigation" closed="true" >}}
Pilotage is navigation by reference to landmarks or checkpoints. It is a method of navigation that can be used on any course that has adequate checkpoints, but it is more commonly used in conjunction with dead reckoning and VFR radio navigation. The pilot visually identifies landmarks outside the aircraft and compares their position with their position on a chart relative to the plotted course line.
{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Dead Reckoning" closed="true" >}}

Dead reckoning is navigation solely by means of computations based on time, airspeed, distance, and direction. The products derived from these variables, when adjusted by wind speed and velocity, are heading and ground speed (GS). The predicted heading takes the aircraft along the intended path and the GS establishes the time to arrive at each checkpoint and the destination. Except for flights over water, dead reckoning is usually used with pilotage for cross-country flying.

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="Checkpoint Selection Factors" closed="true" >}}

Appropriate checkpoints should be selected along the route and noted in some way. These should be easy-to-locate points, such as large towns, large lakes and rivers, or combinations of recognizable points such as towns with an airport, towns with a network of highways, and railroads entering and departing.

Normally, choose only towns indicated by splashes of yellow on the chart. Do not choose towns represented by a small circle—these may turn out to be only a half-dozen houses. (In isolated areas, however, towns represented by a small circle can be prominent checkpoints.)

{{< /details >}}

{{< details title="VFR Cross-Country Route Planning Factors" closed="true" >}}

- **Airspace**—Various classes of airspace and requirements to operate within; avoidance of certain Special Use airspace such as restricted areas, alert areas, CFAs, TFRs, active MOAs.
- **Airports**—Availability of airports or suitable landing areas in case of an emergency or need for diversion, availability of fuel and services.
- **Terrain and obstacle clearance**—Can the aircraft safely overfly, or must it divert around it? Also, avoidance of rugged or hostile terrain.
- **Navigation/communication capability**—Availability of landmarks; VOR signal reception if using VOR navigation; communication availability for contacting FSS, ATC, etc.
{{< /details >}}
