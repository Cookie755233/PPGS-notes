## Student Pilots

- *Q1: WHAT ARE THE WEATHER AND OPERATING LIMITATIONS FOR A STUDENT PILOT?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.89(a), FTA FOM 6.3.2
    - Day visibility: at least **3 SM**.
    - Night visibility: at least **5 SM**.
    - FTA requires visibility to be **greater than 5 SM**.
    - Flight must be conducted with visual reference to the surface.
    - No passengers, compensation or hire, business operations, or international flights.
    - Must comply with all instructor limitations entered in the logbook.

- *Q2: WHAT ARE THE PRE-SOLO REQUIREMENTS?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.87(b), (c), (d)
    - Complete the required aeronautical knowledge training.
    - Pass the instructor-administered pre-solo knowledge test.
    - Receive the required pre-solo flight training.
    - Demonstrate proficiency in the required maneuvers and procedures.
    - Receive an instructor endorsement before solo flight.

- *Q3: WHAT SOLO ENDORSEMENTS DOES A STUDENT PILOT NEED?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.87(n), (o), (p)
    - Solo endorsement must be entered in the student’s logbook.
    - Applies to the specific make and model of aircraft.
    - Valid for **90 days**.
    - Night solo requires additional:
        - Night takeoff, landing, approach, and go-around training.
        - Night navigation training near the airport.
        - A separate night solo endorsement.
    - The instructor must determine the student is proficient before authorizing solo flight.

## Solo Cross-Country

- *Q4: WHAT IS CROSS-COUNTRY TIME?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.1(b)
    - Includes a landing at a point other than the departure point.
    - Uses pilotage, dead reckoning, radio, electronic, or other navigation.
    - For aeronautical experience requirements, the landing point must be **more than 50 NM straight-line distance** from departure.
    - Must be conducted in an appropriate aircraft.
    - Used toward private, commercial, and instrument experience requirements.

- *Q5: WHAT IS REQUIRED BEFORE A STUDENT MAY FLY SOLO CROSS-COUNTRY?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.93(a)
    - Receive cross-country training from an authorized instructor.
    - Demonstrate proficiency in cross-country maneuvers and procedures.
    - Complete the required pre-solo maneuvers under FAR §61.87.
    - Obtain the required solo cross-country endorsements.
    - Comply with all limitations placed in the endorsements.

- *Q6: WHAT IS REQUIRED FOR A SOLO FLIGHT TO AN AIRPORT WITHIN 25 NM?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.93(b)(1)
    - Receive training at the destination airport.
    - Training must include flight in both directions.
    - Include traffic-pattern entry and exit, takeoffs, and landings.
    - Hold a current solo endorsement.
    - Receive an instructor endorsement authorizing the flight.
    - Flight must be for practicing takeoffs and landings.

- *Q7: WHAT IS REQUIRED FOR REPEATED SOLO CROSS-COUNTRY FLIGHTS WITHIN 50 NM?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.93(b)(2)
    - Receive training over the route in both directions.
    - Include traffic patterns, takeoffs, and landings at the airports used.
    - Hold a current solo endorsement.
    - Hold a solo cross-country endorsement.
    - A separate endorsement is **not required for every repeated flight** on the approved route.

- *Q8: WHAT ENDORSEMENTS ARE REQUIRED FOR A SOLO CROSS-COUNTRY FLIGHT?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.93(c)
    - Solo cross-country endorsement for the aircraft category.
    - Endorsement for the specific make and model.
    - Instructor review of the student’s flight planning.
    - **Flight-planning endorsement for each applicable flight.**
    - Instructor must confirm the student is prepared to conduct the flight safely.

## Private Pilot Privileges

- *Q9: MAY A PRIVATE PILOT ACT AS PIC FOR COMPENSATION OR HIRE?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.113
    - Generally, **no**.
    - A private pilot may carry passengers but may not be paid for acting as PIC.
    - Permitted exceptions include:
        - Flight incidental to a business or employment.
        - Pro-rata expense sharing.
        - Charitable, nonprofit, or community-event flights.
        - Search and location operations.
        - Aircraft demonstration by a salesperson.
        - Glider towing and certain light-sport production flight tests.

- *Q10: WHAT EXPENSES MAY A PRIVATE PILOT SHARE WITH PASSENGERS?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.113(c)
    - Fuel.
    - Oil.
    - Airport expenditures.
    - Aircraft rental fees.
    - The pilot must pay at least the pilot’s **pro-rata share**.
    - The arrangement may not be used as disguised compensation.

- *Q11: WHAT CONDITIONS APPLY TO THE PRIVATE-PILOT COMPENSATION EXCEPTIONS?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.113(b), (d)–(h)
    - Business flight must be only incidental to the business.
    - No passengers or property may be carried for compensation or hire.
    - Search-operation reimbursement must be authorized by an agency or search organization.
    - Aircraft salesperson must have at least 200 hours of logged flight time.
    - Charitable flights must comply with FAR §91.146.
    - Each exception is limited to its specific regulatory conditions.

## Medical Certificates and BasicMed

- *Q12: WHICH MEDICAL CERTIFICATE IS REQUIRED FOR EACH PILOT CERTIFICATE?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.23(a)
    - Airline transport pilot privileges as PIC: **first-class medical**.
    - Commercial pilot privileges: at least **second-class medical**.
    - Private, recreational, and student pilot privileges: at least **third-class medical**.
    - A higher-class medical may be used for lower-level privileges.
    - The operation being conducted determines the required medical class.

- *Q13: WHAT ARE THE BASICMED ELIGIBILITY REQUIREMENTS?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.23(c)(3), FAR Part 68
    - Hold a valid U.S. driver’s license.
    - Have held an FAA medical certificate after **July 14, 2006**.
    - Comply with all restrictions on the driver’s license.
    - Complete the BasicMed medical education course every **24 calendar months**.
    - Complete a medical examination with a state-licensed physician every **48 calendar months**.
    - Retain the course certificate and medical examination checklist.

- *Q14: WHAT AIRCRAFT AND FLIGHT LIMITATIONS APPLY UNDER BASICMED?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.113(i)
    - Aircraft authorized for no more than **7 occupants**.
    - Maximum certificated takeoff weight: **12,500 lb**.
    - No more than **6 passengers**.
    - Maximum altitude: **18,000 feet MSL**.
    - Maximum indicated airspeed: **250 knots**.
    - Flight outside the United States requires authorization from that country.

- *Q15: MAY A STUDENT PILOT FLY WITHOUT AN FAA MEDICAL CERTIFICATE?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.89(d), FAR §61.23(c)(3), FAR §61.113(i)
    - Yes, when meeting the applicable BasicMed requirements.
    - Must hold a valid U.S. driver’s license.
    - Must satisfy the medical course and examination requirements.
    - Must comply with all student-pilot limitations.
    - If BasicMed and student-pilot restrictions conflict, the student-pilot restrictions apply.

## Pilot Logbooks

- *Q16: DOES A PILOT HAVE TO LOG EVERY FLIGHT?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.51(a)
    - No.
    - Must log training and experience used toward:
        - A certificate.
        - A rating.
        - A flight review.
        - Recent-flight-experience requirements.
    - Other flights are not necessarily required to be logged.
    - Required entries must include the applicable flight and aircraft information.

- *Q17: WHEN MAY A STUDENT PILOT LOG PIC TIME?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.51(e)(4)
    - Must be the sole occupant of the aircraft, except for the limited airship provision.
    - Must hold the required solo endorsement.
    - Must be training for a pilot certificate or rating.
    - Must operate within all solo limitations.
    - A student does not log PIC merely for manipulating the controls with an instructor aboard.

## Type Ratings, Category, and Class

- *Q18: WHEN IS A TYPE RATING REQUIRED?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.31(a)
    - Large aircraft over **12,500 lb** maximum certificated takeoff weight.
    - Turbojet-powered airplanes.
    - Powered-lift aircraft.
    - Other aircraft specifically designated by the FAA.
    - The pilot must also hold the appropriate category and class ratings.

- *Q19: WHAT IS THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN A TYPE RATING AND AN ENDORSEMENT?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.31
    - Type rating is required for certain large, turbojet, or specifically designated aircraft.
    - Complex airplanes normally require a **complex endorsement**.
    - High-performance airplanes require a **high-performance endorsement**.
    - A high-performance airplane has an engine of more than **200 horsepower**.
    - Endorsements do not replace required category, class, or type ratings.

- *Q20: WHAT ARE AIRCRAFT CATEGORY AND CLASS?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §1.1
    - **Category:** broad classification, such as airplane, rotorcraft, glider, or lighter-than-air.
    - **Class:** classification within a category with similar operating characteristics.
    - Airplane class examples:
        - Single-engine land.
        - Multiengine land.
        - Single-engine sea.
        - Multiengine sea.
    - Example: airplane is the category; single-engine land is the class.
    - Aircraft certification category has a different meaning, such as normal, utility, or transport.

## Flight Review and Recent Experience

- *Q21: WHAT IS REQUIRED FOR A FLIGHT REVIEW?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.56
    - Required every **24 calendar months** before acting as PIC.
    - Minimum **1 hour of ground training**.
    - Minimum **1 hour of flight training**.
    - Includes review of Part 91 rules and required maneuvers and procedures.
    - Requires an instructor logbook endorsement confirming satisfactory completion.
    - Student pilots with a current solo endorsement are exempt while undergoing training.

- *Q22: WHAT IS REQUIRED TO CARRY PASSENGERS DURING THE DAY?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.57(a)
    - At least **3 takeoffs and 3 landings** within the preceding **90 days**.
    - Pilot must be the sole manipulator of the controls.
    - Must be in the same category, class, and type if required.
    - Tailwheel landings must be made to a **full stop**.
    - A pilot may fly solo to regain currency if otherwise qualified.

- *Q23: WHAT IS REQUIRED TO CARRY PASSENGERS AT NIGHT?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.57(b)
    - At least **3 takeoffs and 3 landings to a full stop** within the preceding **90 days**.
    - Must occur from:
        - **1 hour after sunset**.
        - Until **1 hour before sunrise**.
    - Pilot must be the sole manipulator of the controls.
    - Must be in the same category, class, and type if required.
    - Day passenger currency does not automatically provide night currency.

## Change of Address and Deviations

- *Q24: WHEN MUST A CERTIFICATE HOLDER NOTIFY THE FAA OF AN ADDRESS CHANGE?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.60
    - Must notify the FAA after changing the permanent mailing address.
    - After **30 days**, certificate privileges may not be exercised until notification is made.
    - Applies to pilot, flight-instructor, and ground-instructor certificate holders.
    - A residential address must be provided when a post-office box is used.

- *Q25: WHEN MAY A PILOT DEVIATE FROM AN ATC CLEARANCE OR A REGULATION?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §91.123(a), FAR §91.3(b)
    - ATC clearance deviation is permitted when:
        - An amended clearance is obtained.
        - An emergency exists.
        - Responding to a TCAS resolution advisory.
    - A PIC may deviate from a regulation during an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action.
    - Deviation is limited to the extent necessary to meet the emergency.
    - The FAA may request a written report.
    - If a clearance is unclear, immediately request clarification from ATC.

- *Q26: WHAT IS THE PIC’S GENERAL RESPONSIBILITY AND AUTHORITY?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §91.3(a)
    - PIC is directly responsible for the operation of the aircraft.
    - PIC is the final authority regarding the aircraft’s operation.
    - PIC must ensure the flight is conducted safely and legally.
    - Emergency authority does not remove the duty to exercise sound judgment.

## Right-of-Way

- *Q27: WHAT ARE THE BASIC RIGHT-OF-WAY RULES?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §91.113
    - Aircraft in distress has priority over all other aircraft.
    - Head-on: both aircraft turn **right**.
    - Converging: aircraft on the **right** has right-of-way.
    - Overtaking: overtaking aircraft passes to the **right**.
    - Landing: lower aircraft generally has right-of-way.
    - No pilot may use right-of-way rules to create a collision hazard.

## Alcohol and Drugs

- *Q: WHAT ARE THE CONSEQUENCES AND REPORTING REQUIREMENTS FOR ALCOHOL- OR DRUG-RELATED OFFENSES?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §61.15
    - The FAA may deny an application for up to **1 year after the date of final conviction**.
    - A motor vehicle action includes the suspension, cancellation, or revocation of a driver’s license, or a conviction related to operating a vehicle:
        - While impaired by alcohol or drugs.
        - While under the influence of alcohol or drugs.
    - A certificate holder must submit a **written report to the FAA within 60 days** of each motor vehicle action.
    - Failure to report may result in:
        - Denial of an application for a certificate, rating, or authorization for up to **1 year after the motor vehicle action**.
        - Suspension or revocation of an existing certificate, rating, or authorization.

## Oxygen, Restraints, and Parachutes

- *Q28: WHEN IS SUPPLEMENTAL OXYGEN REQUIRED?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §91.211, AIM 8-1-2
    - Above **12,500 through 14,000 feet cabin altitude**:
        - Required crew must use oxygen after **30 minutes**.
    - Above **14,000 feet cabin altitude**:
        - Required crew must use oxygen continuously.
    - Above **15,000 feet cabin altitude**:
        - Oxygen must be provided to every occupant.
    - These are legal minimums; oxygen may be advisable at lower altitudes.

- *Q29: WHEN MUST SEAT BELTS AND SHOULDER HARNESSES BE WORN?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §91.105, FAR §91.107, FTA FOM 7.3.8
    - Required flight crewmembers must wear seat belts while at their stations.
    - Shoulder harnesses must be worn during takeoff and landing when installed.
    - Occupants must be briefed on their use.
    - Occupants must be properly secured during surface movement, takeoff, and landing.
    - FTA requires crewmembers to wear both from before engine start until after engine shutdown.

- *Q30: WHEN IS A PARACHUTE REQUIRED?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §91.307
    - Each occupant must wear an approved parachute when intentionally exceeding:
        - **60° of bank**; or
        - **30° nose-up or nose-down pitch**.
    - Exceptions include required certificate or rating maneuvers.
    - Certain maneuvers performed with an authorized instructor are also exempt.
    - The parachute must meet applicable packing and inspection requirements.

## Night and Aircraft Lights

- *Q31: WHAT IS THE FAA DEFINITION OF NIGHT, AND WHEN ARE POSITION LIGHTS REQUIRED?*
    - **Reference:** FAR §1.1, FAR §91.209
    - **Night:** end of evening civil twilight to beginning of morning civil twilight.
    - Used primarily for logging night flight time.
    - Position lights are required from **sunset to sunrise**.
    - Night passenger currency uses a different period:
        - 1 hour after sunset to 1 hour before sunrise.
    - These three time periods must not be confused.
